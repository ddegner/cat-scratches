It has been nearly a month, as of writing, since a fire broke out at the Navotas Sanitary Landfill in Navotas City on April 10. Yet pockets of fire continue to smolder across the site, with thick smoke still billowing over surrounding communities.

Based on a satellite image captured on April 26, there has been so far an estimated 31.95 hectares of burnt area.

To put that in perspective, 31.95 hectares is roughly equivalent to about 760 basketball courts.

Smoke from the fire has spread beyond Navotas.

In the weeks since the fire began, health authorities have urged residents to wear N95 masks to limit exposure. Air quality has repeatedly deteriorated, at times reaching levels considered unsafe, especially for vulnerable groups.

The smoke also forced the evacuation of residents from two island barangays in Obando, Bulacan — barangays Salambao and Binuangan — located about a kilometer from the landfill.

These barangays are home to 6,890 residents, but only 480 evacuated to mainland Obando. Many chose to stay behind to sustain their livelihoods.

Among them is the husband of Mary Grace Crisostomo, a fisherman who remained in their village while she and their baby stayed in an evacuation center at Obando National High School.

“Kung nandito po siya, wala rin po siyang \[kabuhayan\] dito. Kaya nag-stay siya doon \[sa Barangay Saranbao\],” Mary Grace told Rappler.

(If he’s here, he will not have a livelihood here. That’s why he decided to stay in Barangay Saranbao)

When Rappler visited the evacuation site at Obando National High School on May 4, the smell of smoke from the burning landfill still lingered.

Fishing has also become more difficult. According to 51-year-old fisherman Larry Manalaysay of Barangay Binuangan, thick smoke has made some areas inaccessible.

At times, visibility drops to zero, he said, making it impossible for boats to see each other. Some even get trapped in baklad or fish enclosures.

Like many others, Manalaysay chose to stay to keep his livelihood afloat.

On May 4, Obando authorities allowed residents to return after air quality improved to “fair.” For some evacuees, it meant long-awaited reunions.

Mary Grace said she was finally able to return home and care for her husband.

But conditions remain volatile.

The night before residents were cleared to return, air quality worsened again, according to Wilma Capistrano, a resident of Barangay Binuangan. She described it as the worst since the fire began.

Winds carried smoke back toward their community, she said. Wilma, who has asthma and an enlarged heart, had previously left Obando to stay in Metro Manila to avoid the smoke. She only recently returned.

If conditions worsen again, she said she may have no choice but to leave once more.

“Aalis ako dito kasi, wala eh, ‘yung quality ng hangin dito iba talaga. Makakapatay talaga,” Wilma said.

(I’m leaving this place because the air quality here is really different. It can actually kill.)

At night, the Navotas landfill is illuminated by pockets of fire.

One death has been recorded as of April 27. The Office of Civil Defense (OCD) said the victim died of acute respiratory failure, with aspiration pneumonia as the immediate cause.

At least 195 people have reported health issues linked to the fire, based on April 25 data from the OCD.

Reported Health Issues

Among the reported illnesses is chemical pneumonitis, a severe lung condition caused by inhaling toxic substances.

At least 269 individuals have undergone assessment for toxic exposure, though results have yet to be released.

If even one test returns positive, authorities may be forced to order another round of evacuations in barangays Salambao and Binuangan, said Obando disaster official Cris Jonell Mempin.

The municipality of Obando has already declared a state of calamity on April 28, Councilor Dren De Ocampo confirmed to Rappler.

Efforts to extinguish the fire remain difficult.

Unlike typical blazes that can be put out with water, the Navotas landfill fire is “no ordinary fire,” according to Bureau of Fire Protection spokesperson Anthony Arroyo.

Arroyo said using excessive water is not an option as it could trigger waste runoff into Manila Bay.

"It's not a typical fire with surface combustion or a blaze.... There \[are\] layers of rubbish in a mountainous area, and below that...methane gas," Arroyo said.

Marine conservation group Oceana also expressed concern over the possible contamination of water near the Navotas landfill.

Oceana campaigns and science specialist Diuvs de Jesus said pollutants that are released and travel with smoke plumes settle on land and water.

The landfill’s location on an isolated island further complicates access for heavy equipment. Initially, the equipment was transported using barges.

To address this, a temporary road was built to allow the entry of trucks and machinery.

The Department of Public Works and Highways has been deploying soil cover to suppress the smoke.

But progress is slow. Authorities say steep piles of waste risk collapsing, making it dangerous to fully cover burning sections.

A Japan Disaster Relief expert team flew to the country to provide technical assistance.

As of Tuesday, May 5, about 79.6% of the affected area has been covered.

Authorities still cannot say when the fire will be completely extinguished.

The main cause of the fire has also yet to be determined.

In 2016, it took authorities nine days to extinguish a fire at Mumbai’s largest landfill in India; it is four times larger than the Navotas landfill. At least 70 schools were forced to close due to health concerns, the Washington Post reported.

Meanwhile, a landfill fire in Maalaea, Hawaii, in 1998 burned for four months.

Locally, Cebu’s Inayawan landfill fire in 2019 took nearly 11 hours to extinguish. The facility is about half the size of the Navotas landfill.

The Department of Environment and Natural Resources (DENR) is eyeing legal action against Phil Ecology Systems Corporation (Phileco), which used to operate the Navotas landfill.

“We are not only responding to the smoke. We are also looking at why this happened, what standards may have been violated, and what must be corrected so communities are not put at risk again,” Environment Secretary Juan Miguel Cuna said in a statement on May 3.

At the time of the fire, the landfill was already in the possession of San Miguel Aerocity Inc., which took over the property following a court-approved expropriation.

Phileco’s franchise to operate the landfill expired in August 2025.

Despite ceasing operations, Phileco remains obligated under the Ecological Solid Waste Management Act to implement a safe closure and rehabilitation plan, including a 15-year post-closure care period.

Prior to the fire, DENR had issued notices of violation against Phileco.

The Environmental Management Bureau in Metro Manila said Phileco did not comply with the required daily soil cover, lacked gas vent pipes supposed to release methane gas, and delayed submission of their safe closure and rehabilitation plan.

San Miguel Aerocity plans to develop the area in connection with the New Manila International Airport, located a few kilometers from the landfill.

Since the Navotas landfill was closed down, garbage collected from Metro Manila has been diverted to the New San Mateo Landfill in Rizal. **– with reports from Iya Gozum/Rappler.com**
