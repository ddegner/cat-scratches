### A Review of Ladies Almanack

##### Lauren LeBlanc

During my final semester of college, I read James Joyce’s _Ulysses_. When we were several chapters deep into the notoriously complex novel, my professor assigned the first chapter of James Joyce’s _Finnegans Wake_. The contrast between the two novels made _Ulysses_ feel like a book club selection. Following this instructive line of comparative reading, I should have paused while tackling Djuna Barnes’s well-known 1936 novel, _Nightwood_, to read her slim but daring 1928 work, _Ladies Almanack_. The book is a pastiche of Restoration drama and _Gossip Girl_, and has such an experimental form that it’s not hard to understand why it never reached a wide audience. But Joyce is celebrated in the modernist canon. Why isn’t Barnes’s novel also venerated within that tradition? With its reissue, Dalkey Archive seems to be asking the same question.

Like a typical almanac, the book consists of twelve sections, one for each month of the year, but rather than weather forecasts and moon phases, the chapters are full of meandering musings, overblown language, and a dizzying carousel of larger-than-life characters. Most notable among them is Dame Evangeline Musset, a figure closely drawn from the life of the lesbian American expatriate writer and saloniste Natalie Barney. Barney’s Parisian literary salon, along with her torrid love life, inspired the book and its saucy observations on human nature and behavior. But this is no slice-of-life depiction of Musset’s social milieu. Cheekily named characters (like one called Patience Scalpel) surface in the narrative through waves of conversation but without contextual description. Rather than a coherent plotline, there are opaque philosophical musings and abrupt declarations in ecstatic prose (“I am my revenge!”). Taken together, these difficult-to-parse fragments construct a stage where women live and love independently of men.

The book is so hard to understand, in part, because it was originally intended to be a convalescent gift for Barnes’s partner, Thelma Wood. Though it was published privately in small circulation in 1928, and later reissued in 1972, only readers with an intimate understanding of 1920s Parisian lesbian society could fully decode its veiled references and episodic banter on the nature of love, the body, and time. And even they may have had trouble with some of the inside jokes and sly insinuations. Imagine a fragmented literary romp full of bawdy allusions inspired by gossip from your snarkiest group chat. At the time of the 1972 reissue, all three _New York Times_ staff book critics refused to review it, deeming it indecipherable.

Despite its challenges, there’s much to explore. With its aphoristic bent, _Ladies Almanack_ engages playfully with dense moral and sexual topics, distinguishing it from _Nightwood_, which was written out of Barnes’s frustration with Wood less than a decade later. _Nightwood_’s hallucinatory portrait of a seedy underworld populated by lesbians and outcasts reads as a cautionary tale of desire. By contrast, _Ladies Almanack_ is a stealth bagatelle, bending language to its whims as it feverishly chronicles the characters’ lust, longing, and elation.

To successfully surrender to the book’s colorful language, one should read it slowly, and preferably aloud. The fluid writing style evokes the masked ways that women, specifically queer women, must inhabit the world if they wish to live freely. Barnes seems to say that they can’t be rendered through the clipped, stark prose of Hemingway. They require another form of expression, an aesthetic that can also act as a shield.

Unfortunately, today Barnes’s grand visions of bodily autonomy, free speech, and social acceptance of queer love remain not simply elusive but under attack. In the Dalkey edition’s introduction, lesbian historian Sarah Schulman stresses that the affirmation of queer desire is essential in a world hostile to it. Then and now, _Ladies Almanack_ can be seen as a defense of life, a talisman that—as Barnes argues in the book’s signature arch tone—“all Ladies should carry about with them.”
