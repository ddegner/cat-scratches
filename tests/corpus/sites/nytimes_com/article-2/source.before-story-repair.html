Lakers forced the Thunder into an off night — and still lost. What can they do?

Austin Reaves was 3-for-16 shooting, including 0 for 5 on 3-pointers, in Game 1 against Oklahoma City. Joshua Gateley / Getty Images

By Dan Woike
May 6, 2026 8:00 am EDT

OKLAHOMA CITY — In a lot of ways, this was a worst-case scenario for the Los Angeles Lakers. Game 1 had come and gone, and they sat in the locker room afterward thinking about the opportunity that was there and the ways it got fumbled.

To be clear, the final score wasn’t close. The Lakers lost 108-90 to a better team that had a better night. And, multiple team sources said, the Lakers understand that their best night won’t be good enough to beat the Thunder on anything close to Oklahoma City’s best night.

But after the Lakers did enough Tuesday to make sure the Thunder didn’t have their best night, they still didn’t win. Frustration filled the visiting locker room, powered by thoughts of “what if?”

The Lakers held Shai Gilgeous-Alexander to 18 points and forced the likely back-to-back Most Valuable Player into seven turnovers, but were sometimes a half-step slow out of their traps. They got hit with 10-0 runs and other lightning strikes of offense from the Thunder and didn’t fold, but when it came time for them to hit the big shot to turn a mini-comeback run into a sprint, the ball didn’t go in.

“When we made game plan mistakes, it bit us,” Lakers coach JJ Redick said.

Those mistakes were on the defensive end primarily, Redick said. The Lakers lost too many shooters, gave up too many offensive rebounds and committed too many turnovers.

“You’re playing the world champs, your margin for error in terms of mistakes is not high,” Redick said. “You make mistakes — basketball is a game full of mistakes — it was just too many tonight. We’ve got to clean that up.”

An optimist would look at the Lakers after one game this series and say that LeBron James and his team showed they could make the defending champs uncomfortable, able to force them into non-paint 2-point shots and create enough turnovers with their pressure and double-teaming. A pessimist would say that didn’t matter, and the Thunder will adjust to what the Lakers threw at them with the element of surprise now gone.

That was the vibe postgame. The Lakers were convinced that beating the Thunder isn’t impossible — just incredibly difficult.

“They’re going to test you, obviously,” James said. “They’re going to see how many times they can make you have game plan breakdowns or game plan discipline, and we had a few. Almost too many, obviously, versus a team like this.”

James was hyper-efficient against the Thunder defense. He scored 27 points on 12-for-17 shooting. The Lakers were outscored by just six in his 36 minutes. Leaning on him as a scorer against Thunder defenders he can bully — basically anyone other than Luguentz Dort — was a strategy worthy revisiting.

The Lakers hope that Austin Reaves’ 3-for-16 game was merely another step back in his return to full strength after he missed nearly four weeks. And Marcus Smart showed earlier these playoffs he’s capable of more than combining with Reaves to shoot just 7-of-31 from the field and 2-of-13 from 3. With the Lakers down 6 in the third quarter, both had open looks to make it a one-possession game and both missed.

“I felt like we got some good looks,” Reaves said. “We just didn’t make them.”

Game 2 won’t be as simple as that. The Lakers will need to find new ways to confuse the Thunder and enough poise to withstand a hostile crowd. And they’ll need to be closer to perfect with their defensive assignments, likely without Jarred Vanderbilt.

Vanderbilt suffered a dislocated right pinkie, team sources confirmed, with photos and video appearing to show the joint having punctured the skin. He suffered the injury trying to block a Chet Holmgren dunk, doubling over in pain after smacking the finger off the glass.

Lakers medical personnel covered the finger with a towel and brought Vanderbilt immediately to the locker room, where building staffers could hear his pained screams.

“He’s tough as nails. So if Vando was in pain, then something actually really happened,” James said. “So, it sucks.”

The biggest test for the Lakers will be mental. They know that if they had been better on offense and even more disciplined on defense, they could’ve stolen a game.

“We started out great. We were doing very well, we were in the game,” Smart said. “We held them to 101, 105-ish before late in the game. We’re playing the defending champs, it’s gonna be tough. They’re going to make some tough shots, some tough plays, and we just can’t let that derail us, right? We got to stick with it.”
