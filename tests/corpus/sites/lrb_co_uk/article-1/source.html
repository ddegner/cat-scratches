Something Broken or Nothing at All

Raha Nik-Andish

Share on BlueskyShare on FacebookShareEmailPrint
0

The boiler at home hadn’t been working properly for some time. The water never got more than warm and a slow leak dripped underneath. I called my cousin, a heating engineer. He said he could come in an hour. When he arrived, I asked why he wasn’t at work. ‘They didn’t renew my contract,’ he said. ‘After 44 days of war, the company cut the staff in half.’

He took the cover off the boiler and peered inside. ‘I was earning 120 million rials a month,’ he said, ‘but couldn’t live on it.’ (It’s less than £70.) ‘I took on extra jobs. Air conditioners, boilers – whatever came up.’ He put down his screwdriver. ‘I worked seven days a week just to stay alive.’ He picked the tool up again. ‘I sent a message to my brother in Germany. I told him: I’m stuck here, my life is wasted – save yourself. Now when we talk, I can hear it in his voice. He’s happy. That’s enough for me. People here are changing. More than before, they’re thinking about how to get ahead; how not to be one of the losers.’

He said the boiler could be fixed but it would be better to replace it. He put the call to the supplier on speakerphone. We were told a new boiler would be around 700 million rials (nearly £400). He hung up. ‘With this economy,’ he said, ‘you end up choosing between something being broken and not having it at all.’ While he was working to repair the boiler, I asked after his family. His hands stopped for a moment. ‘My father died two weeks ago.’

‘Why didn’t you let us know?’

‘The cost,’ he said. ‘If we told people, we would have had to hold ceremonies: third day, seventh, fortieth … We couldn’t afford it.’ He tightened a screw. ‘We buried him ourselves. Very simply.’

After he’d got the boiler working again, I asked my cousin to drop me at a café. My back had been hurting for some time. The doctor told me not to drive, so I stopped working for Snapp! Going to the café once a week is a respite from life’s worries. Ten of us sat around a table outside. For a while we talked about books. Then Mohammad, an art professor, turned to me. ‘What did you do about your classes? Are you still teaching?’

I shook my head. ‘No. Everything had to be online for a course that’s supposed to be workshop-based.’

‘I’ve been teaching online,’ he said. ‘But the quality is terrible. I feel like I’m misleading myself and the students.’

‘The department head told me they’re afraid of students being physically present,’ I said. ‘They think protests could start again.’

Mohammad poured tea.

Bahareh had been arrested during last year’s protests and spent forty days in prison. Her voice rose at the other end of the table. ‘This isn’t about getting poorer any more,’ she was saying to Reza. ‘This is absolute poverty. People don’t have bread. When the internet was cut, all my income disappeared. Eggs are a luxury in our house now. My child hasn’t eaten meat for months. And you’re still talking about gradual reform?’

The table went quiet.

‘Everything has doubled,’ Amin said. ‘A sheet of MDF was 70 million rials last week, now it’s 140 million’ (nearly £80).

Mitra, a photographer, described a group exhibition she had taken part in six months ago. According to her agreement with the gallery, if a work sold, 30 per cent would go to the artist and 70 per cent to the gallery. Two of her photographs were sold. She had not been paid. Her phone calls to the gallery went unanswered. When she finally got through, the manager said that buyers had either not paid or returned the pictures. The gallery was struggling to cover its rent.

‘I’ve had no income at all,’ Mitra said. ‘If I didn’t live with my family, I don’t know how I would manage.’

Instability has become a permanent condition. Under such circumstances, trust between employer and worker, buyer and seller, institution and individual gradually weakens, and then disappears.

At around 9 p.m. I left the café with Reza, who gave me a lift. One of the main streets was blocked. Groups of Basij militia were marching, with their black and green flags, religious slogans and yellow banners with military symbols. Loudspeakers played martial music. I said goodbye to my friend and got out of the car.

A woman in a tight green outfit, resembling a military uniform, walked past me. Her face was covered; only her heavily made-up eyes were visible. Further ahead, she clambered onto a military vehicle and stood motionless behind a mounted gun. She looked past the crowd, not at it. Most women on the march were wearing chadors, though a few were not. One was in an unusually short dress. We looked at each other briefly. Her gaze was direct, without hesitation. A woman in a chador leaned towards her and said something. I looked away. Further ahead, another woman stood on a car and spoke through a microphone. She spoke about the dead, about loyalty, about continuing.

When I tried to take a photograph, an officer intervened. ‘Don’t photograph the bombed building,’ he instructed me. ‘Only the crowd.’

Nearby, food was being distributed. Some people sat and ate; others were laughing. For part of the crowd at least, the event was less a political meeting than a social gathering.

On the other side of the street, everyday life continued. Young women strolled past, not wearing hijabs. A line of young people had formed outside a fast-food shop, queuing for falafel and samosas. A hamburger costs around seven million rials (nearly £4), beyond the reach of many Iranians.

At a greengrocer’s, the prices were listed by the half kilo. I bought a few apples and oranges. It had been a while. When I got home I turned on the TV. A young woman, without a hijab, stood among Basij forces, waving a flag. She held up a glass: ‘I drink to the health of our new leader.’ She paused and smiled. ‘I don’t usually drink. But this is for the fighters – and for everyone, whatever they believe, as long as they’re not traitors. A person may drink, may be flogged – but they must not be a traitor.’
